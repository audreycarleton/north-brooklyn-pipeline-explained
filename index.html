National Grid is building a pipeline through Brooklyn.
Here's everything you need to know.


By: Audrey Carleton

Public utility company National Grid is four-fifths of the way through construction on a pipeline that runs through predominantly Black and brown communities in North Brooklyn. But activists say it's not too late to stop it.

The Metropolitan Reliability Infrastructure (MRI) project, more commonly known as the ‘North Brooklyn Pipeline’ would carry fracked gas through Brownsville, Bedford-Stuyvesant, Bushwick, Williamsburg, and Greenpoint to a National Grid Depot on Newtown Creek. Construction for the pipeline began in 2017 — the first three phases have been completed, the fourth is currently underway, and community activists are fighting against the fifth, which National Grid has yet to receive approval on. The proposed fifth phase would cost $185-million in ratepayer funds to accomplish; the company is currently undergoing a rate case with the State to receive permission to hike New Yorkers’ fees pay for it.

FrackTracker estimates show that nearly 200,000 New Yorkers reside within the evacuation zone of the pipeline, alongside a number of schools and 80 daycare centers. All of these Brooklyn residents would be at risk were the pipeline to leak or explode. But these at-risk residents are not those that are being served by the pipeline, many in opposition to it argue, including New York City Public Advocate Jumaane Williams. In fact, living near oil and gas infrastructure is well-known to cause adverse health effects, including preterm birth, asthma, fatigue, migraines and chronic rhinosinusitis. The pipeline is also environmentally damaging: It's at risk of leaking methane, a greenhouse gas that contributes an estimated 86 times more to atmospheric warming than carbon dioxide.

Here's how the numbers on the North Brooklyn Pipeline shake out.

$185-million

How much National Grid is lobbying the State to raise taxpayer rates in order to pay for the pipeline.






153,000

The number of Brooklyn residents who live within the 1,275-foot evacuation radius of the North Brooklyn pipeline, should it leak or explode. 81 daycare facilities, 55 public schools, nine health care centers and three nursing homes also fall within this zone.






1,200

The number of incidents that have taken place at oil and gas pipelines throughout the U.S. since 2010, according to data from the U.S. Department of Transportation’s Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration.






6

The number of years hydraulic fracking has been banned in the state of New York. Activists opposed to the pipeline believe there is a disconnect between the state allowing fracked gas to flow through it having long since banned the practice on its own turf.






11,125

The number of Brooklyn residents who have signed a petition calling on the state to deny permits for construction on the pipeline and ensure that gas never flows through any part of it. Though the hearings are confidential, sources say a decision on the pipeline won't be made until November, 2020.






78

The percentage of residents in Brownsville - where the pipeline begins - who are Black. Advocates say building the pipeline through predominantly low-income neighborhoods of color is environmental racism.
